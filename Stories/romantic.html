THE LAST CIGARETTE

The rain had been falling for three days straight, turning the narrow lanes of old Mumbai into rivers of regret. I stood under the broken awning of the same chai tapri where we first met, a half-burnt cigarette between my fingers. It was our ritual — one cigarette, two people, endless promises.

Her name was Aadhya. She had eyes like monsoon clouds and a laugh that could make even the most broken man believe in tomorrow. We were twenty-two when we fell in love. She wanted to become a writer. I wanted to become someone worthy of her words.

For four years we built our little world in a 10x10 rented room in Andheri. We fought over burnt Maggi, laughed at stupid memes at 3 AM, and made love like the world was ending. She would trace my scars with her fingertips and whisper, “These are just stories waiting to be told.”

Then came the diagnosis. Stage four. The doctors spoke in numbers and percentages, but all I heard was the sound of my future collapsing. She never cried in front of me. Instead, she started writing letters — one for every day she had left. I still have 187 of them, sealed in a metal box under our bed.

The night she left, the rain was merciless. She held my hand with whatever strength remained in her frail body and said, “Promise me one thing… when you smoke our last cigarette, don’t cry. Just remember how beautifully we burned together.”

I kept that promise for 312 days. Tonight is the 313th. The cigarette is the very last one from the pack we bought together on our third anniversary. My hands are shaking as I light it. The smoke curls up like her laughter used to.

“If love could be measured in cigarettes, ours would have burned the entire city down.”
— Aadhya, Letter #184

I take the final drag, feeling the heat reach my fingertips. The rain continues to fall, washing away everything except the ache that refuses to leave. Somewhere in the distance, I swear I hear her laugh again — soft, teasing, full of love.

Some fires don’t die.
They just learn to live inside your chest forever.
