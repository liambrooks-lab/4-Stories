THE WRONG NUMBER

It started with a single missed call at 2:47 AM. Unknown number. I ignored it. Then came the text: “Are you still awake?”

I replied, thinking it was an old friend. “Who is this?”

The reply was instant: “Someone who knows you’re alone right now. Your lights are still on. Third floor, left window.”

My blood ran cold. I lived on the third floor. The left window was my bedroom. I looked outside. The street was empty. No one was there.

For the next seventeen days, the messages never stopped. They knew what I ate for dinner, what show I was watching, even the exact time I brushed my teeth. Every detail was perfect. Too perfect.

One night the message read: “You left the gas on again. Be careful, Rudra. Wouldn’t want you to burn before I get there.”

I changed my number. Got a new SIM the very next morning. That same evening, a new text arrived from yet another unknown number: “Nice try. But I like the new number better. It suits you.”

The messages started describing things that hadn’t happened yet. “Tomorrow at 6:12 PM you’ll drop your coffee on the white shirt you hate. Don’t wear it.” I wore it anyway. At exactly 6:12 PM, the cup slipped from my hand.

Last night the final message came: “I’m tired of watching from afar. Tomorrow I’ll be inside the house. Leave the back door unlocked. We need to talk about why you never noticed me before.”

“I’ve been living in the walls for months now.
You just never listened closely enough.”

I sit here typing this with every light in the house on. The back door is locked. But I can hear soft breathing behind the wardrobe. Slow. Patient. Familiar.

The phone just vibrated.
One new message.
“I’m already inside.”
